U.S. Raids Venezuela, Claims to Capture President Maduro

President Trump announced that U.S. forces had captured President Maduro and his wife following a pre-dawn raid on the capital, Caracas.

Overview

At around 2:00 a.m. on January 3 (1:00 p.m. Hanoi time), Venezuela's capital Caracas was shaken by a series of loud explosions, along with numerous low-flying aircraft and helicopters. U.S. and international media later reported that Washington had launched a large-scale strike on Venezuela after months of both political and military pressure on the government of President Nicolás Maduro.

According to the Associated Press (AP), the strike lasted about 30 minutes. Images and videos circulating on social media and verified by multiple news agencies showed several Venezuelan bases being attacked with long-range missiles and rockets launched from armed helicopters.

The Venezuelan government accused the United States of being behind the strikes on Caracas and three provinces—Aragua, Miranda, and La Guaira— aimed at regime change and seizing the country's resources. The statement also said President Maduro had declared a nationwide state of emergency, ordered full mobilization, and called on the international community to intervene.

Defense Minister Vladimir Padrino López later announced the deployment of the military under Maduro's orders to resist foreign military presence and urged the public to remain calm.

However, the U.S. missile and rocket strikes were likely a cover for a more daring special forces raid targeting President Maduro. Minutes after Minister López's statement, U.S. President Donald Trump posted on Truth Social that the U.S. had successfully carried out a large-scale strike on Venezuela, and had captured President Maduro and his wife, removing them from the country.

Unnamed U.S. officials told CBS that Delta Force, one of the most elite units in the U.S. military, conducted the raid in Caracas and captured the presidential couple.

In later remarks to the media, Trump said the operation had achieved its objective of capturing Maduro alive, describing it as carefully planned, involving outstanding personnel, but did not provide further details.

Republican Senator Mike Lee, citing Secretary of State Marco Rubio, explained that the U.S. deployed forces to Caracas to protect law enforcement personnel executing an arrest warrant for Maduro, who is wanted by the U.S. on charges of collaborating with notorious criminal groups such as Tren de Aragua and the Sinaloa Cartel. The U.S. had offered a $50 million reward for information leading to his capture.

Venezuelan Vice President Delcy Rodríguez acknowledged that the government did not currently know the whereabouts of Maduro and his wife, and demanded proof that they were still alive.

Interior Minister Diosdado Cabello, one of Maduro's closest allies, called on citizens to trust the leadership in Caracas and "not assist invading enemies."

Under Venezuela's Constitution, if the president is unable to govern, power temporarily transfers to the vice president. However, the current situation is considered unprecedented, as the U.S. does not recognize the legitimacy of Maduro's government, while the opposition claims that exiled former lawmaker Edmundo González is the legitimate president.

Colombian President Gustavo Petro called for an emergency meeting of the United Nations and the Organization of American States, while Cuba, Iran, and Russia strongly condemned what they described as a military invasion by the United States.

Since September 2025, the U.S. has significantly increased its military presence in waters off Venezuela, carrying out dozens of operations targeting boats accused of drug trafficking at sea, as well as striking one of the country's ports. Earlier this week, President Maduro said he was ready to cooperate with the U.S. after weeks of pressure, but received no response from Washington.
